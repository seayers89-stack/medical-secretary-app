Self-employment basics

Are you self-employed or employed?

Most secretaries working across several consultants on a freelance basis are self-employed (a "sole trader") rather than employed by any one practice. HMRC looks at the reality of how you work, not just what your contract says. You're more likely to be genuinely self-employed if:

  • You can choose which jobs to take on, and turn work down
  • You decide how and when the work gets done, within reason
  • You can send someone else to do the work in your place (substitution)
  • You're not entitled to holiday or sick pay from the consultant or practice
  • You take on the financial risk of the work — for example, you'd have to fix mistakes in your own time

If a practice controls your hours, tools, and exactly how the work is done in the way an employer would, you may actually be an employee for tax purposes regardless of what either side calls the arrangement.

Getting set up

Registering as self-employed

If you're working for yourself, you need to register for Self Assessment with HMRC — this is how you tell them you're self-employed and need to pay tax through a tax return rather than PAYE.

  • Register online at gov.uk as soon as you start trading, and by 5 October following the end of the tax year in which you started, at the latest
  • You'll be given a Unique Taxpayer Reference (UTR) — keep this safe, you'll need it every year
  • Most secretaries operate as sole traders rather than setting up a limited company — simpler admin, though it's worth discussing with an accountant once your income grows
Staying on top of it

Record-keeping & Self Assessment deadlines

Keep records of everything you earn and spend on the business — most secretaries find a simple spreadsheet is enough at this stage.

  • Every invoice you raise, and proof of when you were paid
  • Business expenses: equipment, software subscriptions, training, mileage, a proportion of home-working costs
  • HMRC generally expects records kept for at least 5 years after the 31 January submission deadline

Key Self Assessment dates for most self-employed secretaries:

  • 5 October — register for Self Assessment if this is your first year trading
  • 31 January — file your online tax return and pay any tax owed for the previous tax year
  • 31 July — second "payment on account" towards the current year's tax, if applicable
The question everyone asks

Does IR35 apply to me?

IR35 is tax legislation aimed at people who supply their services through their own limited company (sometimes called a "personal service company") but who'd otherwise look like an employee of the end client. If you're working as a sole trader — not through a limited company — IR35 generally doesn't apply to you at all.

IR35 becomes relevant if you set up a limited company to invoice consultants or practices through. In that case, whoever you're contracted with may need to assess your employment status for each engagement, and if it falls "inside IR35," tax and National Insurance get deducted similarly to employment, even though you're working through a company.

If you're considering a limited company structure, this is exactly the kind of decision worth a conversation with an accountant first — the right structure depends on your income level, how many practices you work with, and how each engagement is actually set up.

One more threshold to know

Do you need to register for VAT?

You only need to register for VAT once your taxable turnover goes over the current VAT registration threshold in any rolling 12-month period (check the current figure on gov.uk, as it's reviewed periodically). Below that threshold, registration is optional. Most secretaries starting out won't need to think about this immediately, but it's worth keeping an eye on your turnover as your client list grows.
